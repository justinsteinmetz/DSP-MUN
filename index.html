About the Programme

What is DSP MUN?

"When individual countries pursue national interests, how does the world pursue common ideals?"

DSP MUN is a three-unit Model United Nations programme run through DSP English Hub for students in grades 10–12. Over 38 lessons and approximately 16 weeks, students move from the historical origins of the United Nations to conducting their own fully simulated committee session — in English.

The programme integrates academic English skills — research, persuasive writing, formal speaking, and critical reading — with deep engagement in global issues, international law, and diplomatic procedure. Students graduate the programme able to write a structured position paper, deliver a formal speech, draft resolution clauses, and participate in a live committee session.

This guide is the student's single reference point for the entire programme — lessons, vocabulary, rubrics, procedure, and tools — in one place.

Critical lens
The UN through a sharper eye

Understanding the UN's structure is not the same as accepting it uncritically. Strong MUN delegates — and strong analytical thinkers — engage with the institution's contradictions and limitations.

Power structure
The Veto Problem
The five permanent members of the Security Council — China, France, Russia, UK, USA — each hold an absolute veto over binding resolutions. This reflects the power balance of 1945, not 2025. The P5 structure has paralysed the Council on Syria, Ukraine, Gaza, and dozens of other crises where a permanent member has interests at stake.
Critical question: If any P5 member can block any resolution, can the Security Council ever act against the interests of the powerful?
Historical legacy
Colonial Legacies
The UN was founded primarily by Western powers at the height of colonial empire. Many current member states — and the problems they face — are direct consequences of colonialism. The principles of sovereignty and self-determination were applied selectively: rapidly for some, not at all for others. Much of the Global South views the UN system as encoding, rather than correcting, colonial-era power structures.
Critical question: Can an institution designed by colonial powers genuinely represent a post-colonial world?
Institutional design
Implementation Gap
The UN can pass resolutions but has no independent enforcement mechanism. It relies on member states to implement what they have agreed to — and states frequently do not. The General Assembly's resolutions are non-binding. The Human Rights Council has been dominated at times by states with poor human rights records. UN peacekeeping has been implicated in serious abuses in multiple conflicts.
Critical question: Is a body that cannot enforce its own decisions genuinely effective, or does it provide cover for inaction?
Unit 02 of 03 14 lessons · 6 weeks
Research to Policy
Essential question: What is each country's role in addressing global challenges?
Every nation plays a part when the world confronts significant challenges, but the nature and extent of involvement vary dramatically. These differences emerge from a complex intertwining of historical experience, economic capability, political system, cultural values, geographic realities, and national interests. Understanding why a nation chooses a particular path requires recognising these influences.
Research Pipeline

From issue to position paper

Every position paper follows the same intellectual journey. Five stages, each building on the last. Don't skip ahead.

Stage 1
Issue
Define the global problem. What is it, who does it affect, and why does it matter now?
Stage 2
UN Action
What has the UN and international community already done? What resolutions, treaties, or programmes exist?
Stage 3
Country Policy
What is your assigned country's official position? Find voting records, speeches, and government statements.
Stage 4
Solutions
What specific, actionable proposals are consistent with your country's policy and the committee's mandate?
Stage 5
Position Paper
Synthesise all four stages into a structured four-section document ready for committee.

Who gets to define the problem?
Source bias
Where does your research come from?
The most accessible sources on any global issue tend to be published in English, by Western-based organisations. This shapes which stories get told, whose data gets cited, and which solutions get proposed. Actively seek out sources from affected regions — government statements, regional bodies, local civil society.
Question to ask: Am I reading about this issue, or am I reading what powerful actors say about this issue?
Representation
Whose country policy is easier to find?
Research is much easier for well-resourced countries with strong diplomatic missions and established foreign policy infrastructure. For many Global South countries, finding official positions requires deeper digging — but doing so makes your paper substantially stronger and more honest.
Question to ask: If my country is harder to research, what does that tell me about global power structures?
Unit 03 of 03 17 lessons · 7 weeks
Simulated Committee
Essential question: How effectively do UN committees solve global problems?
The structured procedures of UN committees are designed to ensure an orderly, inclusive, and equitable framework for global dialogue. While this system allows for global participation and the development of international norms, effectiveness is often a complex balance — the pursuit of consensus can be hampered by competing national interests, power dynamics, and procedural intricacies.

Critical lens · Simulation
What does MUN not teach?
Simulation limits
The gap between simulation and reality
In MUN, delegations eventually reach consensus because the simulation requires it. In reality, the UN Security Council has been deadlocked for years on Syria, Ukraine, and climate finance. Consider: what does a simulation that always produces a resolution teach you about a system that often doesn't?
Reflection: Which elements of your simulation most accurately reflect real UN diplomacy — and which are smoothed over for pedagogical purposes?
Who is in the room
Representation in practice
Real UN committees include hundreds of delegations, many speaking in their second or third language, with vastly different levels of resources. Western-educated delegates often dominate proceedings regardless of what the voting rules say. Your simulation simplifies this — but the question of who gets heard is always worth asking.
Reflection: Whose voices were heard most in your committee, and why?
